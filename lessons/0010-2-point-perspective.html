Lesson 0010: 2-Point Perspective

Draw a building from a corner view — two vanishing points bring it to life.

Phase 3: Perspective & Proportion

Lesson Goal Draw a cube or building using 2-point perspective with accurate vanishing points and vertical alignment.

When to Use 2-Point Perspective

1-point is for front-facing views. 2-point is for corner views — when you see two sides of an object. It's how we naturally see buildings, boxes, and most objects in daily life.

Setting It Up

1

Draw your horizon line across the page.

2

Place two vanishing points far apart — one on the left, one on the right. The farther apart they are, the less extreme the perspective.

3

Draw a vertical line between the two VPs — this is the front corner of your building.

4

From the top and bottom of the front corner, draw lines to both VPs. These are your orthogonal lines.

5

Draw two more vertical lines between the orthogonals — these define the back corners of the left and right sides.

Build It Up

Once you have the basic box, you can turn it into a house or building:

  • Add a roof by finding the center of each wall and drawing a peak toward the VP
  • Add windows and doors aligned with the wall planes
  • Shadow one side for depth (the side away from your light source)
Common Mistake Placing vanishing points too close together. This creates a distorted, fish-eye look. Keep VPs wide apart — in fact, one or both can be off the page. Use masking tape to extend your horizon line if needed.

Quick Quiz

1. 2-point perspective is best for what kind of view?
2. What happens if you place VPs too close together?

Mini-Project: Street Corner

Draw a street corner with at least two buildings in 2-point perspective. One building should be taller than the other. Add windows, doors, and sidewalks aligned with the perspective. Use hatching to shade one side of each building.

Summary

  • 2-point = corner view with two vanishing points on the HL
  • Vertical lines stay vertical; everything else converges to a VP
  • Keep VPs wide apart to avoid distortion
  • The front corner is the starting point — a vertical line between the VPs
