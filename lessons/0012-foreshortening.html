Lesson 0012: Foreshortening

Create the illusion of depth — draw an arm or hand pointing toward the viewer.

Phase 3: Perspective & Proportion

Lesson Goal Draw an arm or cylinder pointing toward the viewer using foreshortening — where the front appears larger and the back smaller.

What Is Foreshortening?

Foreshortening is the visual compression that happens when an object or body part extends toward or away from you. A fist punching toward you looks huge compared to the shoulder behind it. That's foreshortening.

The Cylinder Exercise

1

Draw a cylinder pointing straight toward you — a circle (the front face) with curved sides converging inward to a smaller circle at the back.

2

Now tilt it slightly. The front face stays largest, the back face is smaller, and the connecting lines angle inward.

3

Practice with extreme foreshortening: the front face is very large, the back face tiny, and the sides are very short lines.

Apply to the Arm

1

Sketch a shoulder and upper arm. The upper arm is normal size.

2

The forearm extends toward the viewer: it's shorter (compressed) but the fist at the end is drawn larger.

3

Overlap the shapes — the fist overlaps the forearm, which overlaps the upper arm. Use overlapping to reinforce depth.

Tip The secret to foreshortening: overlap and size contrast. The front part overlaps the back part, and the front part is drawn proportionally larger. Our brain reads this as "coming toward me."

Mini-Project: Punching Arm

Draw a full arm (shoulder to fist) punching toward the viewer. The fist and forearm should be clearly foreshortened. Add overlapping to show depth. Shade it using hatching to reinforce the form.

Summary

  • Foreshortening = compression of depth — front is large, back is small
  • Overlap reinforces the depth illusion
  • Cylinders are the best practice shape for foreshortening
  • Size contrast sells the effect: the closer part is dramatically bigger
