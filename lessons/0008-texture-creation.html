Lesson 0008: Texture Creation

Different strokes for different surfaces — fur, metal, wood, and more.

Phase 2: Shading & Volume

Lesson Goal Draw three different surface textures — fur, smooth metal, and rough wood — using specific stroke techniques.

Texture Is Stroke Language

Every surface has a visual "language" — the way light interacts with it tells you how to draw it. Smooth surfaces have even gradients. Rough surfaces have broken, uneven marks. Your job is to match your stroke to the surface.

Fur / Hair

1

Draw a simple animal shape (a bear silhouette, a cat face).

2

Use short, quick strokes that follow the direction of fur growth. Don't draw individual hairs — draw clumps and tufts.

3

Layer strokes: dark base layer, then lighter strokes on top for highlights.

Smooth Metal

1

Draw a metal object — a spoon, a kettle, or a cylinder.

2

Use smooth blending with sharp, crisp highlights. Metal has high contrast — dark darks next to bright whites.

3

Leave hard-edged white strips for reflections. Use an eraser to create sharp highlight lines.

Rough Wood

1

Draw a wooden plank or log.

2

Use long, wavy parallel lines for the wood grain. Vary line thickness for natural-looking grain.

3

Add knots (ovals with concentric rings) and rough edges with broken, jagged lines.

Tip The secret to texture: observation. Look at the actual surface. Ask yourself: "Are the edges sharp or soft? Is the contrast high or low? Which direction do the marks go?" Then replicate what you see.

Mini-Project: Texture Swatch Page

Divide a page into 6 sections. In each, draw a 5×5 cm square showing a different texture: fur, metal, wood, fabric (woven), stone, and glass. Label each one. This becomes your personal texture reference.

Summary

  • Fur = short strokes following growth direction, layered
  • Metal = smooth blending + sharp, crisp highlights
  • Wood = wavy parallel lines for grain + knots
  • Texture is about matching your stroke language to the surface
