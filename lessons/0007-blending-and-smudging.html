Lesson 0007: Blending & Smudging

Smooth shading with tissue, blending stump, or even your finger.

Phase 2: Shading & Volume

Lesson Goal Create seamless, smooth gradients using blending tools — perfect for soft surfaces like fruit, skin, or sky.

Hatching vs. Blending

Hatching gives you a textured, graphic look. Blending gives you smooth, realistic transitions. Both are essential — the best drawings often use both.

Your Blending Tools

  • Blending stump — rolled paper tool, gives the most control
  • Tissue / paper towel — good for large areas, soft effect
  • Your finger — convenient but adds oils to the paper (use carefully)
  • Cotton bud / Q-tip — good for small areas and details

Smooth Gradient Practice

1

Draw a long rectangle. Fill the left side with heavy graphite (6B pencil) and the right side with nothing (white).

2

Using a tissue, gently rub from dark to light in a sweeping motion. The graphite will transfer, creating a smooth gradient.

3

Repeat with a blending stump for a more controlled gradient.

Fruit Shading Exercise

Round objects like apples, bananas, or oranges are perfect for blending practice. Their smooth surfaces demand seamless shading.

1

Draw the outline of an apple.

2

Identify your light source. Apply graphite to the shadow areas.

3

Blend from dark to light with a stump. Leave a white highlight.

4

Add a subtle cast shadow on the table surface.

Warning Over-blending creates muddy, gray drawings. Use blending for smooth surfaces (fruit, skin, sky) and hatching for textured surfaces (fabric, wood, rough stone). Know when to stop!

Mini-Project: 3 Fruits

Draw three pieces of fruit (apple, banana, orange) using blending for smooth shading. Each should have a clear light source, smooth gradient, and cast shadow. Use a blending stump for the main shading and a tissue for the background shadow.

Summary

  • Blending creates smooth, realistic gradients
  • Use blending stumps for control, tissue for large areas
  • Blend from dark to light in one direction
  • Don't over-blend — keep highlights white and edges crisp where needed
