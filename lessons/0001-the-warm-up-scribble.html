Lesson 0001: The Warm-Up Scribble

5-minute non-stop doodle to loosen your hand and quiet the inner critic.

Phase 1: Foundation & Line Control

Lesson Goal Get comfortable holding your pencil and making marks on paper. Kill the fear of the blank page.

Why Warm Up?

Every artist — beginner or pro — warms up before serious work. Just like an athlete stretches, your hand and eyes need a few minutes to sync up. A warm-up scribble does three things:

  • It loosens your wrist and shoulder (not just your fingers)
  • It trains you to draw with intent rather than hesitation
  • It proves that quantity beats quality in the beginning

Your First Scribble

1

Take your sketchpad and an HB pencil. Set a timer for 5 minutes.

2

Without lifting your pencil, fill the page with continuous loops, zig-zags, spirals, and figure-eights. Do not stop — even if it looks messy.

3

Every 30 seconds, change your pencil pressure: light (barely touching) → medium → heavy (pressing down). Cycle through all three.

4

When the timer goes off, look at your page. You just made marks — that's the start!

Variations to Try

  • Clockwise spirals — then counter-clockwise. Which feels more natural?
  • Zig-zags — small and tight, then big and sweeping (move from your shoulder)
  • Scratchy marks — fast, chaotic lines with varying pressure

Quick Quiz

1. What is the main purpose of a warm-up scribble?
2. How often should you change pencil pressure during the scribble?

Mini-Project: 5-Day Scribble Log

For the next 5 days, do one 5-minute scribble page before any other drawing. Date each page. At day 5, compare the first page to the last — you'll already see looser, more confident marks.

Summary

  • Warm-ups prepare your hand, eye, and mind for drawing
  • Vary pressure from light to heavy — this builds control
  • Move from your wrist and shoulder, not just your fingers
  • Quantity over quality — the goal is to fill the page, not to make it pretty
